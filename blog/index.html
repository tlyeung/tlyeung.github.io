Things I watched in May 2021

May 26, 2021 · 5 min read

I watched a movie "Promising Young Woman" on 4th May. It was the 2021 Oscar winner of the best original screenplay. But I don't think it deserved such honour. It seems average to me. Maybe there were fewer movies last year due to COVID. Some screens reminded me of another movie this year, "I care a lot", and I found them very unrealistic that a woman could scare off angry men that easily. According to my experience, embarrassed men tend to use violence even more. My girlfriend did not like the revenge idea, I however disagreed with her. Revenge is a must when it is needed. Revenge is one of the common themes in many good movies, even among those all time greats such as the Godfather. I give this film a 6/10.

I watched a French documentary, called "The Immortals", on RTHK TV on 13th May. It was about some animals that have powerful recover ability, or simply do not age, or can survive in the most extreme environment. Do you know lobster, a common seafood, is one of the animals that never age? Human is probably the biggest threat to those old giant lobsters, and so to many other animals as well.

I watched a French documentary on RTHK TV on 17th May, it was called "Le génie des arbres". The French title means "The tree genius". Of course it was about trees. Trees are amazing creatures, they have senses like us. We know the fruits from the trees, we know how to make rubber or oil from trees, they are much more sophisticated than those. Trees do not have brains and do not move much, but in certain ways, they are quite similar to animals. Even scientists are still just starting to understand them.

I watched a documentary on RTHK TV on 18th May, it was called " Secrets in our DNA". Today in the era of information, DNA has become one of the most important data. We can know many things with one's DNA and it is almost impossible to change. It is our privacy, and probably our top secret. But unlike other information, such as our social ids, or even the pictures of our faces, our DNA is predictable. Our DNA is inherited from our parents. It has become a trend that people provide their important data to some companies, that are not much restricted from the governments, to try to get something out of those DNA. For example, do they have secret siblings? Do they have a higher risk to have cancer? Where did they ancestors come from? One of the problems is that your lack of privacy concept may lead your relatives, even the one you have never met before, arrested. The documentary showed some murder cases, including the Golden State Killer, solved by the genetic correlations. But if any terrorists or totalitarian regimes control those databases and companies in the future, how can we prevent them, to trace the people or even their relatives, they do not like?

I watched a documentary "My Octopus Teacher" on Netflix on 26th May. It was the Oscar winner of Best Documentary Feature this year. The structure of the documentary was very simple, just monologue and video clips. The story however was so authentic, very touching. It was a story about a man and an wild octopus. Octopuses are such beautiful creatures, they are intelligent and so different from any other animals we familiar with. We know the shapes of human, cats and dogs, horses and cows, even fishes. But Octopuses do not have a shape. They can be like rocks, carols, fishes and sands, everything you can find under the sea. They don't have shape, they can be solid or liquid. In the documentary, the octopus developed a strong bond with the man. The man was a big part of her very limited life. She lived fast and died young. You can see how she grew and learned and survived and laid eggs and died. She was curious and playful. I feel like I have become a friend with that octopus as well. It was a very nice documentary for all ages. I highly recommend it.

I watched (but did not finish) the movie "Mank" on Netflix on 28th May. It was disappointing. I do not oppose black and white movies, some of them are my favourites, e.g. Casablanca. But the production of "Mank" was, I don't know how to say it better, bad. It looks like a youtube video, or the work by a freshman in movie college. If there is a black and white movie, I expect the cinematography should be like "Schindler's List", or "The Artist". "Mank" was nowhere near that standard. I have never watched a worse movie by David Fincher than this.

Botticelli Exhibition Review

March 9, 2021 · 3 min read

An exhibition of Florence's paintings in late 15th century, Early Renaissance, was held in Hong Kong Art Museum. 40 pieces from the Uffizi Galleries were displayed, including 10 from Sandro Botticelli, the most outstanding artist of that time. In fact he was so significant that the name of the exhibition was "The Hong Kong Jockey Club Series: Botticelli and His Times——Masterworks". One of his most famous works, "Adoration of the Magi", was shown in Hong Kong.

Art works are like sports, you have to watch them on site. Those impacts will be discounted severely if you watch them on monitors or TV. So it is very lucky for Hongkongers to enjoy these precious works in close range. It is obvious that the works from Botticelli outshine others' when I watched and compared them last week.

There were some other famous painters in the exhibition as well, such as Filippo Lippi and Filippino Lippi. But Botticelli's works were on different levels, although they have similar style. Botticelli was very good at putting details in his drawings. He was also good at the light, shadow and the transparent parts, you can find those touches in headdress, hair and clothing. Those skills made his work very stereoscopic, we were like "wearing a pair of 3D glasses", said my girlfriend.

The important patron, Lorenzo the Magnificent, also played a key role in the mature phase of the Golden Age of Florence. There would not have Botticelli if there had had no Lorenzo the Magnificent. Lorenzo is the one who creates that era. That era creates Botticelli. It is like wine vintages; good years creates good wines. If you live in a good year, you can achieve much more. For example, in 1927 the Fifth Solvay Conference, it was the best time of physicians. Botticelli was like Einstein in early Renaissance.

We are in a special time as well. We now have the Internet and new technologies. We can create more things to more people in a faster pace. But are we using what we have to create better artworks? Are we doing our best to solve real problems, such as "food waste and hunger at the same time in the world", but not wasting time in ideologies conflicts? We have better cinematic technologies, but we are not making timeless movies like the "Godfather", "Amadeus", "the Shawshank Redemption" etc.?

Are we in a better or worse time?

Gift exchange problem and my solution

January 1, 2021 · 2 min read

It is common to exchange gifts in a Christmas party. Normally people would draw lots. But have anyone thought of a better method?

For n participants who would like to exchange gifts in a party, we know there are n! permutations of random orders of gifts. For some permutations, however, some participants get their own gifts instead of other's. In those cases, they are failed gift exchange cases. Failing to exchange gifts in a party breaks children's hearts.

For permutations of n participants, we can work out that the combinations of failed gift exchange cases is related to the number of participants, i.e.

a(n) = (n-1) * (a(n-1) + a(n-2))#

On the other hand, we also can simulate this by assuming the probability of getting our own gifts as 1/n. The probability of none of participants getting their own gifts is (1 - 1/n)^n. For n tends to infinity, we can get the probability of that is 1/e , or on contrary, the probability of failed gift exchange cases is 1 - 1/e , which is equivalent to a(n) = (n-1) * (a(n-1) + a(n-2)) when n is large.

To reduce effort, and not letting anyone knows the result first to ensure total randomness, thus an algorithm is needed for exchanging gifts.

The algorithm works like this: first all n participants with their own gifts get a random seat in a circle (it has (n-1)! combinations because of the circular arrangement). Then a random number from uniform distributed numbers from 1 to n-1 is picked and participants can pass their gifts clockwise according the number drawn. As a result, only one draw lots for random seats and one pick for a random number is needed.

This method can prevent participants getting their own gifts at the end of the party, and to speed up the gift exchange session, and to make it funnier.

Shall we have universal testing?

August 3, 2020 · 3 min read

Universal Testing has been a topic in Hong Kong for a few days. Doctors and medical professors, including the prominent microbiologist Yuen Kwok Yung, doubted. Dr Yuen said it can only be possible if we can test all 8 million population in Hong Kong in 4 or 5 days. My question is, even if we can handle 2 million testings a day, is it a good approach?

To get millions of test results, we probably will need to use test kits that made in China. Because the amount of antibodies varies, the test result will not be 100% accurate. Some Chinese test kits have a accuracy as low as only 90%. Let's make it 95% accurate for both positive and negative.

Here we have the total accuracies:

positivefalse positive
95%5%
negativefalse negative
95%5%

Let's assume 80,000 of Hong Kong citizens are infected. After we would have tested all 8 millions, how many of the infected people can we identify? Easy, 80,000 x 95% = 76,000. WRONG! There will be also 5% false positive for the uninfected 7,920,000 people. So there will be 7,920,000 x 0.05 + 80,000 x 0.95 = a jaw dropping 472,000 people!!!

It's unlikely to have such a high false positive rate, while as we have read in the news, some patients may not have a high degree of virus or antibodies for accurate negative tests, so let's change the accuracy assumptions as below. It would be the ideal case.

positivefalse positive
99.9%0.1%
negativefalse negative
99%1%

We will find 7,920,000 x 0.001 + 80,000 x 0.99 = 87,120 people infected. But among them, only 80,000 x 0.99/87,120 = 90.9% are really infected. Almost 10% are false positive cases. While 1%, 800 infected people will test negative! The figures I used were ideal already!

You may learn more about Bayes' Theorem. You can also listen to the podcast of famous mathematician and radio presenter Hannah Fry, from 9:05.

As a result I can conclude that, universal testing is useless. It will find many false positive cases, while hundreds false negative cases will be missed.

So what are the right moves? Here are my suggestions.

  1. We test all patients who thought they have got the virus, even with very mild symptoms. So we can detect the newly infected cases and isolate them as soon as possible.
  2. We test the correlations of the confirm cases sooner the better, obviously they are more likely to be infected than any others.
  3. We keep testing high risk population. Such as staff in hospitals, nurses, doctors, etc. If they have got the virus, they will infect the most vulnerable people.
  4. Start random testings. Let's test 500 people randomly, from all walks of life. It will give us a basic idea how the virus spreads in the community. We can do this on monthly basis.
